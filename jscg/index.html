Introduction

In many linear inverse problems, one collects some observation $y$, knowing the parameter-to-data transform, R. $x$ is to be solved. Usually this is done by minimizing the quadratic functional: $$\chi^2 = \| \mathbf{R}x - y \|^2$$ Expanding the expression and dropping term that doesn't depend on x, we get: $$\chi^2 = x^t \mathbf{A} x - 2 y^t \mathbf{R}x$$ Where $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{R}^t \mathbf{R}$.
The expanded quadratic expression is $$ x^t \mathbf{R}^t \mathbf{R} x - x^t \mathbf{R}^t y - y^t \mathbf{R}x + y^t y $$ but because the dot product is symmetric: $$ x^t \mathbf{R}^t y = (\mathbf{R}x)^t y = y^t (\mathbf{R}x) $$ the non-quadratic term can be combined.

Section 1: Vector Spaces

A vector space is one of the most fundamental structures in mathematics, providing the foundation for linear algebra, functional analysis, and much of modern mathematics.

Vector Space

A vector space $V$ over a field $\mathbb{F}$ is a set equipped with two operations:

  • Vector addition: $+ : V \times V \to V$
  • Scalar multiplication: $\cdot : \mathbb{F} \times V \to V$

These operations must satisfy eight axioms including commutativity, associativity, and distributivity.

The beauty of vector spaces lies in their generality. Whether we're working with ordinary vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$, polynomials, or even functions, the same algebraic structure applies.

Examples of Vector Spaces

Common examples include:

  • $\mathbb{R}^n$ - the space of $n$-tuples of real numbers
  • $\mathcal{P}_n$ - polynomials of degree at most $n$
  • $C[a,b]$ - continuous functions on the interval $[a,b]$

Understanding these spaces and their properties opens the door to linear transformations, eigenvalues, and the rich theory that follows.
